ENT Conditions

Comprehensive clinical research from AAO-HNS, ENT UK, NICE and AOI guidelines.

⚠
All information is for educational research. ENT conditions often require examination with specialised instruments (otoscope, endoscope) that cannot be replicated remotely. Never delay assessment for concerning symptoms.

Quick Clinical Reference

Head-Neck Cancer Red Flags

  • Persistent hoarseness >3 weeks (especially smokers)
  • Unilateral otalgia with NORMAL otoscopy (referred pain - often missed)
  • Non-healing mouth ulcer >3 weeks
  • Unilateral nasal obstruction with bleeding/discharge

Sudden Hearing Loss

  • Unilateral loss developing over <72 hours = EMERGENCY
  • Urgent audiogram and ENT assessment needed
  • Steroid treatment most effective within first 2 weeks
  • Delay significantly worsens recovery chances

Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)

  • Trismus (difficulty opening mouth) is key distinguishing feature
  • Uvula deviation, muffled 'hot potato' voice, drooling
  • ENT EMERGENCY - risk of airway compromise
  • Needs urgent drainage + IV antibiotics

India Oral Cancer Risk

  • Very high burden linked to gutka/paan/areca nut use
  • Any non-healing ulcer or white/red patch in tobacco user: urgent referral
  • Opportunistic screening reduces mortality in high-risk populations
  • Integrate tobacco cessation counselling into ENT visits
Hearing & Balance

Ear Conditions

Research on infections, hearing loss, tinnitus, and vertigo from AAO-HNS and ENT UK guidelines.

Otitis Media and Otitis Externa

Acute otitis media: Middle ear infection, most common in children. Otalgia, fever, reduced hearing. Analgesia first-line; antibiotics if severe/bilateral in under 2s/persisting beyond 3 days.

Otitis media with effusion (glue ear): Fluid without acute infection, common cause of childhood conductive hearing loss. Watchful waiting 3 months; grommets if persistent bilateral effusion affecting development.

Otitis externa: External canal inflammation. Pain worse on tragal pressure (distinguishes from otitis media). Topical antibiotic/steroid drops, keep ear dry. Malignant otitis externa (diabetics/immunocompromised) needs urgent referral.

Hearing Loss

Conductive: Wax, otitis media/effusion, otosclerosis, perforation.

Sensorineural (SNHL): Presbycusis, noise-induced, ototoxic drugs, Meniere's, acoustic neuroma.

Sudden SNHL: ENT EMERGENCY - unilateral loss over <72 hours. Urgent audiogram, corticosteroids most effective within 2 weeks of onset.

Tinnitus and Vertigo

Tinnitus: Usually benign; pulsatile or unilateral with asymmetric hearing loss warrants imaging.

Meniere's disease: Recurrent vertigo (20 min-hours) with fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus, aural fullness. Low-salt diet, betahistine first-line.

BPPV: Brief positional vertigo. Dix-Hallpike test for diagnosis, Epley manoeuvre highly effective treatment.

Cholesteatoma

Abnormal skin growth in middle ear, locally destructive, can erode ossicles/bone. Chronic foul-smelling otorrhoea, conductive hearing loss. Surgical removal (mastoidectomy) is definitive treatment - cannot be treated with drops alone.

Breathing & Smell

Nose & Sinus Conditions

Research on sinusitis, allergy, polyps, and nosebleeds from AAO-HNS and ENT UK guidelines.

Rhinosinusitis

Acute: usually viral, self-limiting 7-10 days. Bacterial superinfection suggested by worsening after initial improvement or persisting >10 days with severe symptoms. Chronic (12+ weeks): intranasal corticosteroids mainstay, FESS for medical failures.

Allergic Rhinitis and Nasal Polyps

Allergic rhinitis: intranasal corticosteroids most effective single therapy. Nasal polyps: associated with asthma and aspirin sensitivity (Samter's triad); biologics for severe eosinophilic disease.

Epistaxis

Anterior (90%+, usually self-limiting) vs posterior (more severe, higher risk). First aid: sit upright leaning forward, pinch soft nose for 10-15 minutes. SEVERE/uncontrolled epistaxis with haemodynamic compromise is an EMERGENCY.

Swallowing & Voice

Throat & Voice Conditions

Research on tonsillitis, hoarseness, and swallowing problems from AAO-HNS and NICE guidelines.

Pharyngitis and Tonsillitis

Usually viral; Group A Streptococcus important bacterial cause requiring antibiotics to prevent rheumatic fever (relevant given RHD burden in India). Centor/FeverPAIN criteria estimate bacterial likelihood.

Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)

ENT EMERGENCY. Trismus, muffled voice, uvula deviation, drooling. Needle aspiration or incision and drainage, IV antibiotics. Risk of airway compromise if untreated.

Chronic Hoarseness and Dysphagia

Hoarseness >3 weeks warrants laryngoscopy referral, especially in smokers (urgent 2-week-wait pathway) - to exclude vocal cord pathology or laryngeal cancer. Dysphagia with ALARM features (weight loss, odynophagia, anaemia) needs urgent endoscopy referral.

Head & Neck Conditions

Research on neck lumps, thyroid nodules, and head-neck cancer red flags from AAO-HNS guidelines.

Neck Lumps

Persistent lymphadenopathy (>3-4 weeks), especially firm/non-tender/enlarging in adults over 40, warrants urgent 2-week-wait referral to exclude malignancy. Congenital: thyroglossal duct cyst (midline), branchial cyst (lateral).

Thyroid Nodules

Majority benign. Red flags: rapid growth, hoarseness, fixed/hard nodule, cervical lymphadenopathy, radiation history. FNAC with Bethesda classification guides management.

Head and Neck Cancer Red Flags

Major risk factors: tobacco (smoked/smokeless), alcohol, HPV, EBV. RED FLAGS: persistent hoarseness >3 weeks, unexplained neck lump, non-healing mouth ulcer, unilateral otalgia with normal otoscopy (often missed), unilateral serous otitis media in an adult.

Children's ENT

Paediatric ENT

Research on glue ear, adenotonsillar hypertrophy, and foreign bodies from AAO-HNS guidelines.

Glue Ear and Adenotonsillar Hypertrophy

Glue ear: leading cause of acquired childhood hearing loss. Adenotonsillar hypertrophy: common cause of paediatric OSA (often hyperactivity rather than sleepiness). Adenotonsillectomy for significant OSA or recurrent tonsillitis.

Foreign Bodies

Unilateral foul-smelling nasal discharge suggests foreign body. Button batteries (nose, ear, swallowed) are a PAEDIATRIC EMERGENCY requiring same-day removal - rapid tissue necrosis. Airway foreign body causing choking/stridor is an EMERGENCY.

Sleep-Disordered Breathing

Sleep & Snoring

Research on obstructive sleep apnoea and snoring from AAO-HNS guidelines.

Obstructive Sleep Apnoea

Repeated upper airway collapse during sleep. Risk factors: obesity, large neck circumference, adenotonsillar hypertrophy (children). Diagnosis: polysomnography, AHI determines severity. Management: weight loss, CPAP (gold standard for moderate-severe), mandibular advancement devices, surgery for selected anatomical causes.

ENT Emergencies

🚨
ENT EMERGENCIES — 112 (India) / 999 (UK) / 911 (US): Airway obstruction, epiglottitis, severe epistaxis, sudden hearing loss, button battery exposure, septal haematoma. ALL URGENT.

🚨 Airway Obstruction

Stridor (especially biphasic), drooling, tripod positioning, cyanosis.

  • Epiglottitis: AVOID throat examination without airway control ready
  • Urgent anaesthetic/ENT involvement essential
  • Do not instrument the airway in emergency setting without controlled setup

🚨 Severe Epistaxis

Uncontrolled or posterior nosebleed with haemodynamic compromise.

  • Signs: tachycardia, hypotension, pallor, dizziness
  • Posterior packing or balloon tamponade
  • Urgent ENT involvement for arterial ligation if uncontrolled

🚨 Sudden Hearing Loss

Unilateral loss developing over <72 hours.

  • Urgent audiogram and ENT assessment
  • Steroids most effective within first 2 weeks
  • MRI to exclude acoustic neuroma

🚨 Button Battery Exposure

Nose, ear, or swallowed battery - PAEDIATRIC EMERGENCY.

  • Rapid tissue necrosis from electrical current
  • Same-day removal required regardless of symptoms
  • Do not wait for symptoms to develop

Nasal Septal Haematoma

Blood collection under septal mucoperichondrium after trauma. URGENT same-day drainage needed to prevent cartilage necrosis ('saddle nose' deformity) and abscess. Any nasal trauma should be examined for this.

India Context

ENT Care in India

India-specific ENT burden, treatment access, and AOI guidance.

Burden and Epidemiology

Chronic suppurative otitis media (CSOM) remains significant, particularly in rural/lower socioeconomic populations. Noise-induced hearing loss growing occupational concern. Head-neck cancers (particularly oral cavity) represent very significant burden, strongly linked to smokeless tobacco/gutka/paan/areca nut use - among highest global rates.

Treatment Access

AOI (Association of Otolaryngologists of India) provides national guidance. Significant tertiary ENT care (AIIMS, private hospitals) for cochlear implantation, skull base surgery. National Programme for Prevention and Control of Deafness (NPPCD) improves early identification and subsidised hearing aid provision. PM-JAY provides insurance coverage for major ENT procedures.

Tobacco and Oral Cancer Screening

Opportunistic oral cavity screening by trained health workers has shown mortality benefit in high-risk populations (notably Kerala studies). Tobacco cessation counselling should be integrated into ENT consultations given direct causal link to laryngeal/oral/oropharyngeal cancers.
